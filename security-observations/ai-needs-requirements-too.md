# AI Needs Requirements Too

**Category:** Security Observation  
**Topics:** Artificial Intelligence, Functional Analysis, Governance, Cybersecurity, Risk Management  
**Author Perspective:** Functional Analyst transitioning into Cybersecurity

---

## Overview

One of the most interesting trends in technology today is the rapid adoption of Artificial Intelligence.

Organizations are exploring AI to improve productivity, automate tasks, accelerate decision-making, and create new opportunities for innovation.

The conversation often focuses on what AI can do.

However, an equally important question is:

What requirements should govern how AI is used?

As a Functional Analyst, I have learned that successful solutions rarely begin with technology.

They begin with understanding needs, defining objectives, establishing expectations, and managing risks.

AI should be no different.

---

## Technology Is Not the Requirement

When organizations adopt new technologies, there is often excitement around capabilities and potential benefits.

Questions typically include:

- How much time can this save?
- What tasks can it automate?
- How quickly can we implement it?
- What efficiencies can we gain?

While these questions are important, they do not define success.

Technology can provide functionality, but requirements define how that functionality should be used.

Without clear requirements, organizations may find themselves solving one problem while creating several others.

---

## Questions Worth Asking

Before implementing an AI solution, I believe organizations should understand:

- What business problem are we trying to solve?
- What information will the AI access?
- Who is responsible for the outputs?
- How will results be validated?
- What risks are acceptable?
- What data should never be shared?
- How will usage be monitored?

These questions are not obstacles to innovation.

They are part of responsible implementation.

---

## AI and Security

Many cybersecurity concerns related to AI are not caused by the technology itself.

Instead, they emerge when expectations, responsibilities, and controls are not clearly defined.

Examples include:

- Sensitive information being shared unintentionally
- Excessive permissions being granted
- AI-generated outputs being trusted without validation
- Unclear ownership of decisions
- Lack of governance surrounding acceptable use

These challenges often resemble traditional governance and process issues more than technical failures.

---

## A Functional Analyst Perspective

One lesson I have learned through process management and functional analysis is that requirements create clarity.

Requirements help stakeholders understand objectives.

Requirements establish boundaries.

Requirements create accountability.

Requirements reduce ambiguity.

As AI becomes increasingly integrated into daily operations, I believe organizations will benefit from applying the same discipline used for any other business-critical solution.

Before asking what AI can do, organizations should understand how AI should be used.

The answer to that question often determines whether an implementation succeeds or creates unnecessary risk.

---

## Key Takeaway

Artificial Intelligence introduces powerful capabilities, but capabilities alone do not guarantee successful outcomes.

Technology may provide the solution.

Requirements provide the direction.

Organizations that invest in defining expectations, responsibilities, and governance early in the process are better positioned to adopt AI in a secure, sustainable, and effective manner.

AI may be new.

The need for clear requirements is not.
