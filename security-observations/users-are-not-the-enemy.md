# Users Are Not the Enemy

**Category:** Security Observation  
**Topics:** Cybersecurity, Human Risk Management, Security Awareness, Change Management, User Adoption  
**Author Perspective:** Functional Analyst transitioning into Cybersecurity

---

## Overview

One observation I continue to encounter while learning about cybersecurity is how often users are described as the weakest link in security.

While there is some truth to the idea that human error contributes to many security incidents, I believe this perspective can sometimes overlook a more important question:

Why are users making these decisions in the first place?

Most employees do not arrive at work intending to create security risks.

In most cases, they are simply trying to complete their tasks, meet deadlines, and help the organization achieve its objectives.

When security and usability are in conflict, users will often choose the path that allows them to continue their work.

Understanding that reality is essential to building effective security programs.

---

## Looking Beyond User Error

When a security incident occurs, it is easy to focus on the action that triggered it.

Someone clicked a phishing email.

Someone shared information incorrectly.

Someone used a weak password.

Someone bypassed a security process.

However, focusing solely on the user's action can prevent organizations from identifying deeper issues.

Questions worth asking include:

- Was the security process clearly explained?
- Was the user properly trained?
- Was the secure option also the easiest option?
- Did the user understand the risk?
- Were expectations communicated effectively?

In many situations, what appears to be a user problem may actually be a process, communication, or design problem.

---

## Security Through Enablement

Throughout my experience supporting process improvement and organizational change, I have learned that people are far more likely to adopt new practices when they understand their purpose.

The same principle applies to cybersecurity.

Users should not be expected to follow security requirements simply because they exist.

They should understand:

- Why the requirement exists
- What risk it addresses
- How it protects the organization
- How it protects them

When people understand the reasoning behind a control, compliance often becomes cooperation.

---

## Security and User Experience

Effective security should support users rather than compete with them.

Organizations that successfully balance security and usability often achieve better outcomes because they focus on creating processes that are both secure and practical.

Examples include:

- Simplifying access request procedures
- Providing clear guidance and documentation
- Designing user-friendly security controls
- Offering ongoing education rather than one-time training
- Gathering feedback from users affected by security requirements

Security becomes stronger when it is integrated into the way people naturally work.

---

## A Functional Analyst Perspective

As a Functional Analyst, I spend much of my time understanding user needs, identifying process gaps, and translating requirements into practical solutions.

This experience has reinforced an important lesson:

People generally want to do the right thing.

When users struggle with security controls, the solution is not always stricter enforcement.

Sometimes the solution is better communication, clearer processes, improved training, or more thoughtful design.

Strong security programs recognize that users are not obstacles to overcome.

They are stakeholders whose participation is essential to success.

---

## Key Takeaway

Users are often described as the weakest link in security.

I prefer to think of them as one of the most important components.

Technology can enforce controls.

Processes can define expectations.

But people ultimately make the decisions that determine whether those controls and processes are effective.

Organizations that invest in helping users succeed are often better positioned to build a stronger security culture and reduce long-term risk.
