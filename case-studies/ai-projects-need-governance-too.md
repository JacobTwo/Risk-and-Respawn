# AI Projects Need Governance Too

## Scenario

An organization decides to introduce an AI-powered solution to improve productivity and support daily operations.

The expected benefits are clear:

- Faster information retrieval
- Improved efficiency
- Reduced manual effort
- Better decision-making support

The project moves forward quickly, and attention is focused on functionality, performance, and adoption.

However, several important questions remain unanswered:

- What data can the AI access?
- Who is responsible for validating outputs?
- How should sensitive information be handled?
- What happens if the AI generates inaccurate recommendations?
- How will access be managed and reviewed?

The technology is ready.

The governance framework is not.

---

## The Problem

AI projects are often evaluated based on what the technology can achieve.

Less attention is given to the controls, processes, and responsibilities required to manage the risks associated with its use.

Without proper governance, organizations may unintentionally expose sensitive information, create compliance concerns, or make decisions based on inaccurate outputs.

The challenge is not simply implementing AI.

The challenge is implementing AI responsibly.

---

## Security Impact

Insufficient governance can introduce risks such as:

- Unauthorized access to sensitive information
- Data privacy violations
- Inaccurate or misleading outputs
- Lack of accountability for AI-generated decisions
- Regulatory and compliance concerns
- Increased operational risk
- Reduced trust in AI-assisted processes

These risks often emerge not because the technology fails, but because expectations and controls were never clearly defined.

---

## Root Cause Analysis

Several factors commonly contribute to AI governance challenges:

- Incomplete requirements gathering
- Undefined ownership and accountability
- Limited understanding of AI-related risks
- Absence of usage policies
- Lack of validation procedures
- Pressure to adopt emerging technology quickly

Organizations may focus on implementation while overlooking long-term governance considerations.

---

## Recommended Controls

### Define Clear Requirements

Establish what the AI solution is expected to do, what data it may access, and what limitations should apply.

### Establish Ownership

Assign responsibility for oversight, maintenance, and governance.

### Data Classification

Define which information can and cannot be used with AI systems.

### Human Validation

Ensure critical decisions are reviewed by qualified individuals.

### Access Governance

Apply the principle of least privilege and regularly review permissions.

### AI Usage Policies

Develop clear guidelines for acceptable use, data handling, and accountability.

### Ongoing Risk Assessments

Review AI implementations regularly to identify emerging risks and changing requirements.

---

## Functional Analyst Perspective

One of the most interesting aspects of AI adoption is that many challenges begin long before implementation.

As a Functional Analyst, I have learned that successful solutions depend on clear requirements, stakeholder alignment, and well-defined processes.

The same principle applies to AI.

Before asking what an AI solution can do, organizations should understand:

- What problem are we trying to solve?
- What information will be involved?
- Who is responsible for outcomes?
- How will risks be managed?

Technology can accelerate progress.

Governance helps ensure that progress remains sustainable.

---

## Key Takeaway

AI introduces new opportunities, but it also introduces new responsibilities.

Organizations that invest in governance, accountability, and risk management from the beginning are better positioned to benefit from AI while maintaining security, trust, and operational resilience.

Successful AI adoption is not only a technology initiative.

It is a governance initiative as well.
